Graphs for grants and publications

I produce graphs from data working with researchers and grant seekers to communicate stories with their data. I love that I get to help communicate insights in conservation research that could help inform a richer, more bio-diverse future for our planet. Programing in R is also a fun distraction from my normal design work, and AI tools have only made it easier to work with complex data sets quickly and efficiently.

Average Catch

This graph shows the average catch rate of endangered marine species, many of which are not protected by CITIES and many are trafficked internationally.

Bycatch rates

Here we show how bi-catch rates, which are traditionally measured at the fishery level, are complex and vary vessel to vessel. This understanding can help us reduce bi-catch by penalizing the worst performing boats and encouraging practices from the best boats across the fleet.

Average catch

Below we give an overall picture of bi-catch of IUCN listed vulnerable and endangered species. This allows us to compare the major clades (fish, sharks, and shellfish) against each other by volume, and helps us understand which species are the most at risk.

Spatial catchability

Catchability is a derived value that helps us estimate the likelihood that species will be caught by certain gear types. This is important for fisheries with less data and observation and helps us estimate how these fisheries are impacting species.

Mean catchability

Here relative catchability is shown across three major clades (sharks, mammals, and turtles), and 15 commonly used subdivisions.

Catchability by geartype

This series of polar graphs shows the impact across gear types (here showing Driftnets) and the impact to specific species. This helps us understand which species are most at risk by gear-type.
